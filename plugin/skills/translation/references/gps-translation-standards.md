# GPS Translation Standards Reference

Guidance for handling foreign-language and historical-script records
in accordance with the Genealogical Proof Standard.

## Translations Are Derivative Sources

A translation is a derivative source. The original-language document
remains the original record. When a researcher translates a record,
the translation is one step removed from the original — it reflects
the translator's interpretation of words, phrasing, and meaning.

Consequences for the research pipeline:

- Always preserve and cite the original-language text alongside any
  translation.
- When conflicts arise between a translation and the original, the
  original governs.
- A translation carries additional risk of error beyond what the
  original already carries (scribe error, informant error, etc.).
- Record the translator's identity and qualifications as part of the
  source analysis, just as you would note a derivative record's
  compiler.

## Reading Handwriting Correctly (GPS Standard 23)

Researchers must correctly read all legible handwriting in materials
they consult. For foreign-language records this means:

- **Learn the script before reading the content.** German records
  from the 1500s through 1941 use Kurrentschrift or Sutterlin, not
  modern Latin letterforms. Reading these as Latin letters produces
  systematic misreadings.
- **Use the record's formulaic structure as a constraint.** When a
  character is ambiguous, the record type (baptism, marriage, burial)
  limits the set of plausible words. A baptism register will contain
  "getauft" (baptized), not "gestorben" (died).
- **Flag uncertain readings explicitly.** Mark unclear characters
  with [?] or [illegible]. Never silently guess — especially for
  names, which are the most consequential genealogical data.
- **Distinguish similar letterforms systematically.** In Kurrent,
  common confusion pairs include e/n, u/n, m/nn, f/long-s, k/t,
  and C/E. Work through ambiguous passages character by character.

## Understanding Period Meanings (GPS Standard 24)

Researchers must understand all legible words, phrases, and
statements in the sources they consult — including the meaning for
the source's time and place. For translation work this means:

- **Words change meaning over time.** A term in a 1650 German record
  may not carry the same meaning it does in modern German. Always
  consider the historical and regional context.
- **Legal and ecclesiastical formulae have precise meanings.** Phrases
  like "filius legitimus" or "lediger Stand" are technical terms with
  specific legal implications, not casual descriptions.
- **Regional vocabulary varies.** The same concept may use different
  terms across dialects or jurisdictions. A word used in a Bavarian
  parish register may differ from the Prussian equivalent.
- **Do not impose modern meaning on archaic text.** Translate what
  the scribe meant in context, not what the words might suggest to a
  modern reader.

## Transcription Standards (GPS Standard 29)

When transcribing foreign-language records:

- **Include the entire item.** Transcriptions cover the complete
  record — headings, insertions, notations, endorsements, front and
  back, and any attachments.
- **Annotate damage and illegibility.** Use square brackets or
  footnotes to indicate where a source is damaged, illegible, omits
  expected information, or provides unexpected information.
- **Preserve format when relevant.** When layout matters (column
  headings, tabular registers), reflect the original's format, line
  lengths, and physical features.
- **Mark transcription boundaries.** Clearly identify where the
  transcription begins and ends, so readers know exactly what is
  quoted from the source.

## Rendering Original Text Exactly (GPS Standard 32)

When transcribing or quoting foreign-language sources:

- **Reproduce wording, spelling, and abbreviations exactly.** Do not
  modernize spelling, expand abbreviations silently, or correct
  apparent errors. Show the text as written.
- **Preserve numbering, superscripts, and similar features.** Render
  the text as faithfully as the output format allows.
- **Handle obsolete letter forms correctly:**
  - The long s (also written as a tall form resembling f) is
    transcribed as "s," not as "f."
  - The thorn (an Old English / early Germanic letter for "th") is
    transcribed as "th" or with the thorn character, not as "y."
  - The double-f ligature used as a capital F in some traditions is
    transcribed as "F," not as "ff."
  - When the original letterform is available in the output character
    set, use it. Otherwise use the modern equivalent — never a
    look-alike with a different meaning.
- **Use square brackets for insertions.** Short editorial additions
  go in [square brackets]. Longer commentary goes before or after the
  transcription as clearly separated text or footnotes.
- **Capitalization and punctuation.** Except at the beginnings and
  ends of quotations, show these exactly as they appear in the
  original.

## Foreign-Language Handling (GPS Standard 6 / Chicago Manual)

The genealogical documentation standard follows Chicago Manual of
Style conventions for foreign-language material:

- **Italicize foreign words** that are not proper nouns when they
  appear in English-language narrative text.
- **Do not italicize proper nouns** (personal names, place names)
  even when they are in a foreign language.
- **Quotations in the original language** are not italicized — they
  are placed in quotation marks or block-quote format, same as
  English quotations.
- **Translate for the reader.** When quoting foreign-language text in
  a finished product, provide an English translation — either inline
  in parentheses, in a footnote, or immediately following the
  quotation.
- **Preserve original-language names.** Do not anglicize personal
  names. "Johann" remains "Johann," not "John." Note the English
  equivalent only if it aids understanding.

## Abstracts vs. Transcriptions vs. Translations

These are distinct operations with different rules:

| Operation | What it does | Key rules |
|-----------|-------------|-----------|
| **Transcription** | Reproduces the original text character-for-character in modern type | Exact rendering; annotate damage/illegibility |
| **Abstract** | Condenses the record, omitting formulaic/redundant wording | Quote any phrases of 3+ words from the original; do not modernize names or dates |
| **Translation** | Converts from one language to another | Derivative source; preserve and cite the original alongside |

For genealogical translation work, the typical workflow is:

1. Transcribe the original-language text (following transcription standards)
2. Translate the transcription into English
3. Annotate ambiguous readings, period-specific meanings, and abbreviation expansions
4. Present both the transcription and translation together
