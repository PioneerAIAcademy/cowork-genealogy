# Source Classification Guide

How to classify the source itself -- the container that holds
information. Source classification is Layer 1 of the three-layer
model and is independent of the other two layers (information and
evidence).

## Three Categories

### Original

The first recording of information, or the earliest surviving version.

Characteristics:
- Created at or near the time of the event by someone with direct
  knowledge or official authority
- Digital images, microfilm, and photocopies of originals still count
  as original -- they are faithful reproductions
- Government "record copies" made by a recording office as part of
  its official function (e.g., a deed recorded in a county deed book)
  are treated as original
- Certified transcripts from the custodial agency may qualify

Watch for edge cases:
- A county birth register in alphabetical order may actually be
  derivative -- the alphabetical arrangement suggests entries were
  recopied from loose original certificates
- Census sheets in alphabetical order may have been recopied from
  the enumerator's original returns
- Court copies of wills: derivative if the original still exists;
  if the original is lost, the court copy becomes the earliest
  surviving version

### Derivative

Any record created from another source through copying, transcribing,
abstracting, indexing, or translating. Common types:

- **Index** -- alphabetical listing pointing to entries in a larger
  record. The most common derivative type.
- **Abstract** -- summary omitting some details from the original
- **Transcript** -- word-for-word copy in a new format
- **Translation** -- rendering from one language to another
- **Extract** -- partial quotation of selected portions
- **Database entry** -- information entered from another source

Critical rule: a derivative of a derivative does not make the first
derivative an original. If an index was created from a transcript,
the transcript is still derivative.

### Authored Narrative

Synthesizes information from many sources with the author's own
analysis and conclusions:

- Compiled genealogies and family histories
- Biographies and county histories with biographical sections
- Research reports and proof arguments
- Cemetery books that go beyond listing inscriptions to include
  analysis of symbols, family relationships, etc.

## Why Classify?

The practical purpose is to ensure original records are used whenever
possible. Each step from original to derivative introduces
opportunities for transcription errors, omissions, and
misinterpretation. When using a derivative, always attempt to locate
and verify against the original.

Classification does NOT determine reliability. That assessment happens
at the information layer. An original source can contain unreliable
information; a derivative can contain perfectly accurate information.

## Per-Record, Not Per-Type

You cannot blanket-classify an entire record type. Each individual
record must be examined on its own merits. One census image might be
the enumerator's original handwritten sheet; another from the same
district might be a recopied version.

## Provenance Chain

When classifying, trace the path from original creation to the version
you are examining. Note each step:

1. Who created the original?
2. How was it preserved (courthouse, church, private hands)?
3. Was it microfilmed? By whom?
4. Is the digital image a scan of the microfilm or a direct scan?
5. Is the version you accessed an index, transcript, or image?

Document this chain in the source entry's `notes` field. Example:
"Accessed as digital image on FamilySearch of microfilm made by the
Genealogical Society of Utah from the original register held by
St. Mary's Parish, Cork, Ireland."
