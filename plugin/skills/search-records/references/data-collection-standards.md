# Data Collection Standards for Record Searching

Reference for GPS-aligned data collection during search execution.
Grounded in BCG Standards 19-36 and BYUI research methodology.

## Scope of Collection

Collect all information that could be relevant to the research
question — not just the data you expect to find. Unexpected details
(neighbors, witnesses, associated names, occupations, property
descriptions) often prove critical later during correlation.

Do not let bias or preconception determine what you collect.
Record information that supports AND contradicts your working
hypothesis with equal attention. Evidence that complicates a
hypothesis is just as valuable as evidence that supports it.

## Source Classification at Point of Collection

When you encounter a search result, immediately classify it:

### Original vs. Derivative

- **Original record**: The first recording of information, created
  at or near the time of the event by someone with authority or
  direct knowledge. Examples: a church baptismal register entry, a
  handwritten census schedule, a signed marriage license.
- **Derivative source**: Created from another source — includes
  indexes, abstracts, transcriptions, translations, and database
  entries. An index entry in a search result is always derivative.

**Critical rule**: An index entry is NOT the record. It is a
pointer to the record. When a search returns index entries, the
researcher must attempt to locate and examine the underlying
original record before drawing conclusions.

### Why This Matters for Search

Most FamilySearch search results are index entries — derivative
sources extracted by volunteers or automated systems from original
record images. These indexes:

- May contain transcription errors (misread handwriting)
- May omit information present in the original (partial indexing)
- May standardize names or dates differently than the original
- May lack context visible in the full document (marginal notes,
  adjacent entries, document structure)

Always treat index data as preliminary. Follow through to the
original image or record whenever possible.

## Information Quality Assessment

For each piece of information collected, assess:

1. **Who provided the information?** The informant's relationship
   to the event determines reliability. A parent reporting a child's
   birth has firsthand knowledge; a neighbor reporting the same
   birth may not.

2. **Primary vs. secondary information**: Primary information comes
   from someone with firsthand knowledge of the event. Secondary
   information comes from someone reporting what they heard or
   learned after the fact. A death certificate's cause of death is
   primary (from the attending physician); its birth date is often
   secondary (from a surviving relative who may not remember
   accurately).

3. **Proximity in time**: Information recorded close to the event
   is generally more reliable than information recorded years later.
   A baptismal record created days after birth is more reliable
   for birth date than a death certificate created 80 years later.

4. **Motive and bias**: Consider whether the informant had reason
   to be inaccurate. Pension applications may exaggerate service;
   immigration records may understate age to avoid restrictions.

## Handling Negative Search Results

A search that returns no matching results is still a finding.
Negative results are data — they narrow the space of possibilities
and may constitute negative evidence.

### When absence is meaningful

The absence of a record is analytically significant when:
- The record type was being created in that time and place
- The person should have appeared (e.g., a head of household
  missing from a census in a jurisdiction where they were known
  to reside)
- The collection is reasonably complete for the relevant period

### When absence is NOT meaningful

Absence is less informative when:
- The collection is known to be incomplete or poorly indexed
- The record type did not exist yet in that jurisdiction
- The person would not have been included by definition (e.g.,
  searching military records for someone too young to serve)

### Recording negative results

Log every negative search with the same detail as a positive one.
Include what you searched for, what parameters you used, and why
the absence matters or does not matter. This documentation is
essential for demonstrating reasonably exhaustive research.

## Evaluating Database Quality Before Searching

Before relying on any database or collection, assess it:

1. **What does it actually contain?** Read the collection
   description. A title like "U.S. Marriage Records" may cover
   only certain states and years.
2. **Is it an index only, or does it include original images?**
   Index-only collections require following up to find originals.
3. **What time period and geography does it cover?** Understand
   the boundaries before interpreting negative results.
4. **Who created the index?** Volunteer-indexed collections have
   different error profiles than professionally indexed ones.
   Computer-aided indexing (OCR/HTR) has its own characteristic
   error patterns.
5. **Are there known gaps or limitations?** Some collections
   exclude certain record subtypes, parishes, or years.

## Note-Taking Discipline

When examining search results:

- Record content exactly as it appears in the source — do not
  modernize names, dates, or spellings in your notes
- Clearly distinguish between what the source says and your
  interpretation of what it says
- Note formatting details, column headings, and contextual
  information that may affect interpretation
- If the source is an image, note its condition and legibility
- Capture the full context: adjacent entries, household members,
  marginal annotations

## Evidence Types to Watch For

Search results may provide three types of evidence:

- **Direct evidence**: Answers the research question explicitly
  (e.g., a birth certificate stating parents' names when the
  question is "Who are the parents?")
- **Indirect evidence**: Provides information from which the
  answer can be inferred (e.g., an age on a census record from
  which a birth year can be calculated)
- **Negative evidence**: The expected information is absent from
  a source where it should appear (e.g., a couple not listed in
  marriage records for a county where they were known to reside)

All three types carry equal weight in building a proof argument.
Do not dismiss indirect or negative evidence as less important
than direct evidence.
