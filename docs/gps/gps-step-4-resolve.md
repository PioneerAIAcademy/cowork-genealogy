Step 4 of the GPS: Systematic Resolution of Conflicting Evidence

1. Fundamentals of Evidence Resolution: The Credibility Mandate

Step 4 of the Genealogical Proof Standard (GPS) is the architectural process of substantiating a conclusion's credibility by reconciling mismatching information. In the technical workflow of a Research Architect, a conclusion is not a "discovery" but a verified state achieved only after all contradictory data has been neutralized through logical analysis.

[!WARNING] The Credibility Mandate: A conclusion cannot be considered "proved" or credible if conflicts remain unresolved. Ignoring or dismissing contradictory evidence out of hand violates the GPS. Any remaining ambiguity automatically invalidates the "Proved" status of a conclusion.

1.1 The Iterative Resolution Cycle

Step 4 is not a terminal phase but a diagnostic gate that frequently triggers re-entry into the research loop:

1. Conflict Detection: Identified during Step 3 (Analysis and Correlation).
2. Gap Identification: Step 4 often reveals "Search Gaps"—where the absence of a specific record (e.g., a missing probate for a hypothesized candidate) becomes a critical data point.
3. Recursive Search (Step 1): The discovery of a conflict necessitates a return to a "Reasonably Exhaustive Search" to find the data required for resolution.
4. Final Synthesis (Step 5): Only once conflicts are resolved through a defensible rationale can the agent proceed to a soundly reasoned written conclusion.

2. Conflict Triage: Taxonomy of Genealogical Discrepancies

Research Agents must categorize conflicts to determine the likely root cause before attempting resolution.

Conflict Category	Common Variables	Primary Cause
Identity/Names	Spelling variants, middle name usage, Americanization.	Literacy levels, phonetics, transcription errors, or intentional assimilation (e.g., "Dutch" for "German").
Chronology	Discrepancies in birth, marriage, or death years.	Delayed records, misreporting by informants, or intentional lying (e.g., military/pension fraud).
Jurisdiction	Shifting birthplaces or residences across records.	Political boundary changes (e.g., VA to WV) or informant confusion between origin vs. birth location.
Kinship Labels	Terms like "junior," "in-law," or "cousin."	Historically fluid definitions (e.g., "junior" denoting age, not parentage; "son-in-law" as an adopted son).

2.1 Same-Name Disambiguation Logic

When multiple individuals share a name, the agent must apply the FAN Principle (Family, Associates, Neighbors) to map the subject's social network.

* The Co-enumeration Rule: Co-enumeration in a single record (e.g., two distinct households with the same-named head on the same census page) is definitive evidence of distinct individuals.
* Protocol: Maintain separate data profiles for same-name candidates until a unique identifier (e.g., a specific middle name or land description) forces a merge or definitive separation.

3. The Three-Layer Evaluation Framework

Every assertion must be filtered through this hierarchical logic gate. Analysis occurs at the assertion level; a single document can house varying reliability levels.

3.1 Layer 1: Source Classification (The Container)

Source Type	Definition	Technical Examples
Original	The first recording of an event, created at/near the time.	Official death certificate, deed, family Bible entry.
Derivative	Copies, transcriptions, indexes, or abstracts.	Ancestry.com index, published book of abstracts.
Authored	Compiled works citing other sources.	Family histories, online trees, biographical sketches.

3.2 Layer 2: Information Reliability (The Content)

* Primary: Provided by a direct witness or participant.
* Secondary: Reported by someone who was not a witness (secondhand).
* Indeterminate: Informant unknown. [RED FLAG]: Indeterminate info necessitates a lower confidence tier until the informant's identity or knowledge base can be hypothesized.

3.3 Layer 3: Evidence Type (The Relevance)

* Direct: Explicitly answers the research question.
* Indirect: Implies an answer; requires inference or correlation with other facts.
* Negative: A meaningful absence of information that should reasonably exist.

4. Logical Resolution Gates: Methodologies for Rationale

4.1 Informant Reliability Analysis

Agents must evaluate the Who and Why of a record via a mandatory reliability checklist:

* Relationship: Was the informant a witness (primary) or reporting from memory (secondary)?
* Temporal Proximity: How much time elapsed between the event and the recording?
* Potential Bias: Did the informant have a reason to misreport (e.g., military enlistment, pension eligibility, hiding ethnicity due to prejudice)?

4.2 The Hugh Lee Elimination Logic

For unrecorded parentage or identity, use the Systematic Elimination Protocol:

1. Candidate Identification: Identify all potential candidates (e.g., the "Three Brother" logic).
2. Conflict Comparison: Test each candidate against known parameters (age, migration, religious affiliation).
3. Exhaustive Exclusion:
  * Candidate A: Eliminated due to age discrepancy (e.g., 10 years too young).
  * Candidate B: Eliminated via a probate record/will that names all children but excludes the subject.
4. Logical Remainder: If one candidate remains after an exhaustive search of all possibilities, that candidate is identified by process of elimination.

4.3 The Preponderance Hierarchy

When weighing evidence, prioritize based on this sequence. Crucial Rule: Verify Source Independence. Two derivative sources based on the same original record are a single data point and do not constitute "Multiple Independent Sources."

1. Original sources over derivative.
2. Primary information over secondary.
3. Contemporary recording over later recollection.
4. Official/formal records over casual/informal.
5. Unbiased informants over biased.
6. Multiple independent sources over single sources.

5. Advanced Logic: Negative Evidence and "The Dog Not Barking"

Negative evidence is the inference drawn from the absence of information. This follows the Sherlock Holmes principle: the fact that the dog did not bark is the clue.

* Scenario 1 (Census): IF a child is missing from the census... THEN there is a prima facie case for non-membership or death, which must be corroborated by the absence of that child in the parent's probate records.
* Scenario 2 (Probate): IF a living individual is not named in a parent’s will or obituary while all other siblings are listed... THEN there is high probability the individual is not a biological child or has been legally severed.
* Scenario 3 (Locality): IF an exhaustive search of a specific county’s records (probate, land, tax) yields no trace of a particular surname... THEN the family likely did not reside there during that period, despite oral tradition.

6. Operational Guidelines for AI Research Agents

6.1 Anti-Fabrication Guardrails

* No Merging: Forbid merging identities without co-enumerated proof.
* No Hedging in Proved Status: If the "Proved" threshold is met, the agent must state the conclusion as fact.
* Uncertainty Notation: Mark gaps with [unclear] or [citation needed].

6.2 The Negative Search Log

The agent must log every search yielding "Nil" results.

* Requirement: Include "Reason for Search" and "Specific Parameters" (e.g., Searched Oneida County Probate Index 1825–1850 for 'Lee'—Result: Nil).

6.3 Standardized Confidence Tiers

1. Proved: Requires 2+ independent original sources with primary information and zero unresolved conflicts. No hedging language (e.g., "suggests") is permitted.
2. Probable: Strong evidence, but reliant on derivative or secondary sources.
3. Possible: Hypothesis exists; evidence is thin or conflicting.
4. Not Proved: Insufficient evidence to reach a conclusion.
5. Disproved: Evidence definitively refutes the hypothesis.

6.4 Quality Gate Verification Checklist

* [ ] Conflict Matrix Produced: Have you generated a side-by-side table comparing every conflicting data point?
* [ ] Source Independence Verified: Have you confirmed that "multiple sources" are not just copies of one original?
* [ ] Negative Search Log Complete: Are all "Nil" results recorded with specific parameters?
* [ ] Assertion-Level Analysis: Is each fact (Birth, Death, Parentage) classified by Source, Information, and Evidence type?
* [ ] Defensible Rationale Written: Is there a written, logical explanation for why one piece of evidence was favored over another?
* [ ] Elimination Logic Applied: Have all other reasonable candidates been systematically excluded?
* [ ] Privacy Check: Are living persons protected from disclosure of PII?
* [ ] Confidence Tier Assigned: Does the tier match the rigorous requirements of the GPS?
