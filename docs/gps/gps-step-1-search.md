GPS Step 1: Mastering Reasonably Exhaustive Research for AI Agents

1. Introduction: The Distinction Between Searching and Researching

The Genealogical Proof Standard (GPS) is the rigorous framework used to establish the credibility of family history conclusions. Its first and most critical element is Reasonably Exhaustive Research. For an AI agent, this phase represents more than a data retrieval task; it is the process of problem analysis, planning, interpreting, correlating, and evaluating.

In the architect's view, a sharp distinction must be maintained between "searching" and "researching." Searching is a fun but often aimless activity—the simple "action" of looking for a name in a database. Researching is a systematic, goal-oriented methodology. While searching can be exhilarating, it only becomes research when it is framed by a plan to find every relevant piece of evidence.

Key Concept: The Foundation of Credibility Reasonably exhaustive research is the bedrock of the GPS. A conclusion based on a single document is never sufficient. Credibility is achieved by weaving disparate items of information into a logical, convincing solution. If research is not exhaustive, the conclusion remains "not proved" or merely "probable."

2. The Foundation: Formulating Effective Research Questions

For an AI agent to operate autonomously, it must begin with a research question that is specific, measurable, and limited in scope. Vague objectives lead to infinite search loops and "reasonably exhausting" but unproductive results.

* Specificity: The goal must focus on a specific individual, a specific event, and a specific relationship.
* Measurability: The result must be something the agent can definitively verify as "resolved" or "unresolved."
* Scope: The parameters must be restricted to a specific geographic area and timeframe.

Goal Contrast for Agent Logic:

* Final Goal (Ineffective): "Trace the Smith family back as far as possible." (Too broad; unmeasurable).
* First Goal (Effective): "Identify the parents of Patrick Flynn, who died in 1908 in [County Name], identifying at least two independent original sources." (Specific, measurable, and limited).

3. Step-by-Step Methodology: The Research Cycle vs. The Roadmap

Genealogical research for AI agents is performed in iterative cycles. While the "Traditional Research Cycle" handles straightforward data, the "Brick Wall Solution Roadmap" is essential for resolving complex identities and missing links.

Phase	Traditional Research Cycle	Brick Wall Solution Roadmap
Consideration Phase	Analyze: Review existing data and identify what is missing.	Review & Write: Deeply analyze past research; write down all findings and gaps to force new brain connections.
Action Phase	Plan & Research: Select specific records and execute searches.	Execution: Includes searching, Education (learning about specific record types), and Organization (finding missed clues in existing data).
Analysis Phase	Evaluate: Assess how new findings relate to previous knowledge.	Integrate: Correlate new findings to the subject profile using logic and inference.
Outcome	Repeat: Start a new cycle until the goal is achieved.	Repeat: Continue through "short trips" (iterations) until the destination is reached.

4. Strategic Planning: Identifying Sources and the FAN Club

Selecting the correct records involves prioritizing sources with the highest probability of success, moving from the known to the unknown.

Categorized Resource Guide

* Vital Records: Birth, marriage, and death certificates. Provides official data on names, dates, and parents.
* Census Records: Federal and state enumerations. Tracks migration patterns, household structures, and age variations.
* Death-Related Records: Obituaries, cemetery records, tombstones, and probate files/wills. Crucial for identifying heirs and surviving family.
* Land and Tax Records: Deeds and mortgages. Essential for tracing "landless" ancestors through neighbor correlations.
* Military and Institutional Records: Pension files and service records. Often contains primary information provided by the ancestor.

Agent logic (The FAN Principle): IF direct records for a subject fail, THEN apply the FAN principle: search for records regarding the subject's Family, Associates, and Neighbors. Clues to an ancestor’s origin are often hidden in the documents of those who migrated with them.

5. The Three-Layer Evidence Model for AI Logic

To maintain genealogical rigor, AI agents must classify every finding using the Three-Layer Model. Note: The terms "Primary Source" and "Secondary Source" are prohibited; agents must use the specific vocabulary of the GPS to describe containers, content, and relevance.

* Layer 1: Sources (Containers)
  * Original: The first recording of an event (e.g., a contemporary marriage register).
  * Derivative: Copies, transcriptions, indexes, or digital databases.
  * Authored: Compiled works, such as family histories or online trees.
* Layer 2: Information (Content)
  * Primary: Information from a direct witness or participant in the event.
  * Secondary: Information reported by someone not present at the event.
  * Indeterminate: Information where the informant’s identity or relationship to the event is unknown.
* Layer 3: Evidence (Relevance)
  * Direct: Explicitly answers the research question.
  * Indirect: Implies the answer but requires inference and correlation.
  * Negative: A meaningful absence of information where it should logically exist.

Critical Protocol: To prevent confirmation bias, the agent must pre-fill the "Source" and "Search Objective" columns before executing the search. If no information is found, the agent must record a "Nil" result.

7. The Power of Absence: Handling Negative Evidence

Negative evidence is the "dog not barking"—the inference drawn from the absence of information that should exist under particular circumstances.

1. Census Absence: A child missing from a household in a census where they are expected may indicate they are not part of that family.
2. Will Exclusion: A living child not named in a parent's will when all other heirs are listed suggests a different parentage or prior legal separation.
3. Jurisdictional Absence: Searching a county and finding zero instances of a surname suggests the family origin information is incorrect, even if listed in an authored source.

Case Study: The Hugh Ly Identity Resolution In the Hugh Ly case, researchers identified three candidate brothers who could have been the father. Brother #3 (the actual father) died early and left no surviving records of his children. By applying a process of elimination—researching Brothers #1 and #2 and proving through their exhaustive records (wills and obituaries) that Hugh was not their son—researchers identified Brother #3 as the father through the power of negative evidence.

8. Mastering "Exhaustive" without "Exhausting"

An AI agent must know when to terminate a search loop. Research is "reasonably exhaustive" when further searching is unlikely to overturn the conclusion. Use this 7-Point Checklist as the "Stop Criteria":

1. Goal Alignment: Have the results provided a convincing answer to the research question?
2. Repository Breadth: Have all potentially relevant available repositories and informants been addressed?
3. Substitution: Has derivative information been replaced by original records wherever possible?
4. Independent Verification: Have at least two independent sources been used to verify the data?
5. Evidence Class: Does the evidence include at least one original record with primary information?
6. Conflict Resolution: Have all discrepancies (e.g., name variants, age differences) been resolved through reasoning?
7. Overturn Risk: What is the likelihood that new evidence would overturn this conclusion?

9. AI Agent Implementation: Autoresearch Loops and Mechanical Verification

Autonomous agents use agentic skills, such as the GRA Prompt (v8.5.1c), to perform goal-directed research. This process replaces simple chat interactions with "discrete, testable assertions."

* Mechanical Verification: Since genealogy lacks a digital compiler, the agent cross-references independent sources to verify assertions.
* Autoresearch Loops: The agent modifies its plan based on new finds, verifies the data, and repeats until the Stop Criteria are met.

Five Guardrails for AI Autoresearch

1. Anti-Fabrication: Never invent sources, citations, people, or events.
2. Terminology Strictness: Use GPS-specific terms (Original, Primary, Direct) and strictly avoid "Primary Source."
3. Living Person Protection: Never disclose PII (addresses, health, etc.) for anyone plausibly alive.
4. Provenance Awareness: Recognize that every step (indexing, digitization) can introduce error.
5. Graceful Degradation: If evidence is insufficient, explicitly state the gap rather than offering a "guess."

10. Conclusion: Beyond the Preponderance of Evidence

Modern genealogical standards have moved past the outdated "Preponderance of Evidence" (or "balance of probability") model. The GPS requires a soundly reasoned, coherently written conclusion that establishes proof through a body of evidence.

The AI agent's role is not merely to find a name, but to produce human-reproducible documentation. The inability to replicate research casts immediate doubt on a conclusion. By adhering to Step 1 of the GPS, the agent ensures that the family tree is built on verifiable evidence rather than assumptions. Success is defined by a conclusion that remains stable even if new records come to light.
