GPS Step 5: The Soundly Reasoned, Coherently Written Conclusion

1. Introduction to the Final Element of the GPS

The Genealogical Proof Standard (GPS) culminates in Step 5: the creation of a soundly reasoned, coherently written conclusion. This is the definitive act of substantiating credibility. Without this step, research remains a collection of disparate data points rather than a defensible historical truth. By formalizing our reasoning, we ensure we are accurately "climbing our own family tree" and providing a map that allows future researchers to replicate our steps and reach the same conclusion.

A conclusion is not a conclusion until it is written. Research is a physical process of documentation, not a mental state of "feeling sure." Step 5 transforms "searching" into "researching" by articulating exactly how the evidence supports the identity, relationship, or event in question.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------


2. The Three Vehicles of Proof: Selecting the Right Format

The complexity of your evidence and the presence of conflicting information dictate the "proof vehicle" required. Selecting the appropriate format ensures that the depth of the reasoning matches the difficulty of the problem.

Proof Vehicle	Evidence Conditions	Conflict Status
Proof Statement	Relies on direct evidence from high-quality sources.	No conflict; 2+ independent sources agree.
Proof Summary	Synthesizes multiple sources; often involves indirect evidence.	Minor or superficial conflicts that are easily explained.
Proof Argument	Narrative reasoning for complex cases (e.g., identity resolution).	Irreconcilable or significant conflicts requiring a defensible rationale.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------


3. Structural Mechanics: Statements, Summaries, and Arguments

Every conclusion must be built using specific mechanics to meet the threshold of "soundly reasoned."

1. The Proof Statement

This vehicle is reserved for straightforward assertions where sources agree.

* Methodology Example: In the "Patrick Flynn" case, the conclusion that Patrick is the son of Thomas Flynn is a statement substantiated by correlating a death certificate (which provides secondary information on parentage) with contemporary 1851 and 1861 census returns and Thomas’s probated will.
* Ingredients for Success: Assertion of fact, Citations for 2+ independent original sources, Verification of primary information.

2. The Proof Summary

Used when the case is built on a "cluster of attributes" rather than a single smoking gun.

* Methodology Example: A summary might weave together naming customs, land partitions, and family tradition. While oral tradition is a derivative source, its value increases when it correlates with original records like a tax collector's list.
* Ingredients for Success: Synthesis of disparate data, Explanation of minor variances, Footnoted documentation for every claim.

3. The Proof Argument

This narrative vehicle is the "gold standard" for resolving brick walls, such as the "Thomas Woodson" case study. This masterclass in reasoning used DNA evidence in concert with exhaustive documentary research to resolve the question of whether Woodson was the child of Sally Hemings.

* Methodology Example: A proof argument must address the "dog that didn't bark"—Elizabeth Shown Mills' concept of the "inference we can draw from the absence of information that should exist under particular circumstances."
* Ingredients for Success: Resolution of conflicting evidence, Correlation of the FAN club (Family, Associates, Neighbors), Explicit rationale for setting aside specific data.

4. The Logic of Reasoning: Avoiding Unsound Assumptions

To substantiate a conclusion, the researcher must move beyond stagnant thinking and apply a rigorous hierarchy of evidence.

Checklist for Logical Integrity

* [ ] Distinguish Container from Content: Use the "Container vs. Content" metaphor. The Source is the container (e.g., a deed book); the Information is the content provided by the informant.
* [ ] Apply the Preponderance Hierarchy: Weigh evidence in this strict order:
  1. Original over derivative (if information quality is equal).
  2. Primary over secondary information.
  3. Contemporary recording over later recollection.
  4. Official/formal over casual/informal.
  5. Unbiased over biased informants.
* [ ] Execute Same-Name Disambiguation: Treat same-named individuals as distinct until proven otherwise. Note that co-enumeration in a single record (e.g., two households on one census page) is definitive evidence of distinct persons.
* [ ] Identify Shared Errors: Recognize that multiple online trees repeating the same mistake are not "independent sources"; they are virally copied errors tracing to a single flawed source.

5. Documenting the Resolution of Conflicting Evidence

Step 5 formalizes the resolution of Step 4. You must articulate a defensible rationale for why certain evidence is accepted and other data is set aside.

Rationale Framework for Articulating Choice:

* "The [Source A] is preferred over [Source B] because the informant was a direct witness to the event, whereas the informant for [Source B] was reporting secondhand information decades later."
* "The birth year in [Source C] is considered a contemporary recording, outweighing the later recollection found in the [Source D] obituary."
* "The conflict in parentage is resolved in favor of the [Original Will] as the [Derivative Index] contains a documented transcription error."


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------


6. The Iterative Writing Process: Note-taking vs. Reporting

Writing is not the final act of research; it is an iterative process of consideration.

Research Note-taking (The Action Phase) Capture raw data during the search. Record every source consulted, including "nil" results. This ensures you do not repeat searches and allows you to track the "barking dog" (meaningful absences).

Reporting and Summarizing (The Consideration Phase) Process raw data into usable information. This is where you correlate findings and identify new questions for the next research cycle.

Comparison of Writing Styles:

Raw Research Note: "1880 Census: Mary Cowen, age 19, born in Ohio. Husband George present."

Soundly Reasoned Conclusion: "Although the 1880 census lists Mary Cowen’s birthplace as Ohio, this is an outlier. Three subsequent birth records for her children and her own death record consistently place her birth in Pennsylvania. The 1880 entry likely reflects the informant's residence at the time or a misunderstanding of the question 'where are you from' rather than Mary's actual place of birth."

7. Assessing Confidence Tiers and Final Terminology

Strict adherence to GPS vocabulary is non-negotiable.

Standard Vocabulary:

* Sources (Containers): Original, Derivative, or Authored.
* Information (Content): Primary (eyewitness), Secondary (secondhand), or Indeterminate.
* Evidence (Relevance): Direct (answers the question), Indirect (requires inference), or Negative (meaningful absence).

The Confidence Scale

* Proved: The conclusion is supported by 2+ independent original sources with primary information and no unresolved conflicts. Do not hedge with "suggests" or "indicates."
* Probable: The evidence is strong and a clear preponderance exists, but the "2+ independent original sources" threshold may not be fully met.
* Possible: The conclusion is a viable hypothesis but requires more evidence to move beyond the balance of probability.
* Not Proved: Insufficient evidence to make a determination.
* Disproved: Evidence affirmatively refutes the hypothesis.

9. Final Ethics and Safety Filter

Before publication, apply the following guardrails to protect individuals and descendant communities.

* Living Person Protection: Apply the "plausibly alive" rule. Treat anyone with an unconfirmed death as living if the timeframe (e.g., born within the last 100 years) allows. Omit all PII (Personally Identifiable Information).
* Cultural Competency & Colonial Framing: When analyzing records of historical trauma (e.g., slavery, genocide), recognize the colonial framing of the record creator. Center the humanity of the subject and adhere to CARE principles for Indigenous data sovereignty.
* Sensitive Information: For findings involving criminal records or traumatic deaths, provide content warnings and consider the potential harm to living descendants before public disclosure.
